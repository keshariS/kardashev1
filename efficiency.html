"Final energy" is what actually reaches factories, homes and vehicles. The gap between primary and final is dominated by thermal conversion losses in coal, gas, and nuclear power stations — all of which operate under the same thermodynamic limits.

Efficiency on the K Scale

What conversion losses actually mean for our Kardashev rating

–
K · primary energy
Total input including waste  ·  – TWh
vs
–
K · final energy only
Useful work reaching end users  ·  – TWh

The Kardashev number is defined by total primary energy input — which means conversion waste counts toward the score. Our 33.7% loss rate actually inflates K by ~0.018: a perfectly efficient civilisation delivering the same useful energy would record a slightly lower K, because it would need less total primary input.

K = 1 requires 1016 W — 87.6 million TWh per year, roughly 496× our current total. Efficiency improvements alone cannot close that gap; they reduce primary energy demand without raising raw throughput. The path to K = 1 requires genuinely commanding more energy — solar, wind, fusion — not just wasting less of what we already harvest. Efficiency matters because it makes sustainable growth cheaper and faster, not because it directly advances the K number.

Efficiency by Source

Fraction of raw input that becomes usable electricity or work

For thermal sources (coal, gas, nuclear), efficiency = electrical output ÷ fuel heat content. For direct-electric sources (solar, wind, hydro), captured energy converts to electricity with minimal loss — the "uncaptured" resource continues naturally in the environment.
